Pocket Clinic Inc.

Hardware Design and Validation Engineer | Mar 2025 - May 2025

What I Did & Used

At Pocket Clinic, I worked on electrical circuit design, hardware validation, and troubleshooting for a connected medical-device project. The work connected PCB design, sensing materials, firmware, mechanical packaging, and cost-aware component decisions. Because the device context was medical and privacy-sensitive, the final deliverable keeps proprietary design details generalized while still showing the engineering process behind the testing, research, and validation work.

I researched smart sensing materials and sensor approaches including RFID sensing, PVA, phenanthrene, ZnO thin film behavior, and other material responses that could support reliable detection. I also created Verilog/SystemVerilog testbench concepts for matrix-style PCB pin testing, but those were not used as the project direction shifted toward physical component testing, material behavior, and rubber degradation validation. That testing used staged MOSFET control with Arduino, ATtiny85, and Pro Mini/Nano-style platforms to evaluate whether the material and assembly could hold up under repeated thermal stress.

I reviewed schematic and layout work in KiCad with ERC and DRC checks, helped refine sequential MOSFET control, and researched whether an ESP32 could replace the Arduino Pro Mini for better modularity, wireless logging, Bluetooth/Wi-Fi control, and future scalability. The ESP32 had strong advantages, but the 3.3V logic level created extra complexity for the 5V MOSFET gate requirements, so the lower-complexity Pro Mini path remained the more practical fit for the test setup.

What I developed most from this role was the ability to move between research, simulation, firmware, and real hardware without treating them as separate problems. I learned how quickly a clean idea can change once component limits, material behavior, power constraints, safety, cost, and manufacturability are involved. It made me more disciplined about reading datasheets, testing assumptions on the bench, documenting tradeoffs clearly, and choosing designs that are not just technically interesting, but practical enough to build, debug, and improve.
